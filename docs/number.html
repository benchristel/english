Number

There are two main classes of nouns: count nouns and mass nouns.

Count Nouns

Most nouns are count nouns, which have singular and plural forms. The singular is the form found in the dictionary. To form the plural, suffix -owBLANK#PL.

  • gwydhtree "tree" → gwydhowtree#PL "trees"

Often, the plural suffix causes the final consonant of the word to double (in writing).

  • tarosvanghost "ghost" → tarosvannowghost#PL "ghosts"
  • dwegduck "duck" → dweggowduck#PL "ducks"

Count nouns also have a collective form similar to the derivation bag → baggage. The collective is formed by suffixing -iodBLANK#COLL.

  • dwegiodduck#COLL "duckage"

Collective forms mean essentially "the set of all Xs (under discussion)". For the purposes of agreement, collective forms are singular. For example, you'd refer back to a collective noun using a singular pronoun.

Collective nouns are used when talking about groups of things whose individuality is not important, e.g. eleniodstar#COLL "stars, as a group or phenomenon". They are also used when the number of things under discussion is unknown, e.g. flenniodchild#COLL "child(ren)", cafytiodfurnishing#COLL "furniture". The collective forms can be treated as mass nouns, e.g. theuliodpebble#COLL "gravel" from theulpebble "pebble".

Mass Nouns

All mass nouns in EnglishLANGNAME are formed from adjectives by adding the collective suffix discussed above.

  • oethvilmonster onniron_0#LEN "iron monster" (iron used as an adjective "made of iron")
  • onniodiron_0#COLL "iron (as a material)"
